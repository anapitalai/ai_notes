# Bible Study: 1 Timothy 1 (KJV) — Line-by-Line Contextual Interpretation

> **Translation:** King James Version (KJV)  
> **Book:** 1 Timothy — A pastoral letter written by the Apostle Paul to his young co-worker Timothy, who was overseeing the church at Ephesus.

---

## Background

The First Epistle to Timothy is one of three "Pastoral Epistles" (along with 2 Timothy and Titus) attributed to the Apostle Paul. Paul writes to Timothy, whom he left in charge of the church at Ephesus, to give him instructions on church order, sound doctrine, and personal conduct. Chapter 1 opens with Paul's greeting, a charge to guard against false teaching, a reflection on the true purpose of the Law, and a powerful personal testimony of God's grace toward even the worst of sinners.

---

## Verse-by-Verse Study

---

### Verse 1
> **"Paul, an apostle of Jesus Christ by the commandment of God our Saviour, and Lord Jesus Christ, which is our hope;"**

**Context & Interpretation:**  
Paul opens by identifying himself as an *apostle* — one who is sent with authority. Crucially, he states this is "by the commandment of God," not by his own ambition or the appointment of men. This establishes his divine authorization to write with authority. He calls God "our Saviour," a title reflecting redemption, and Jesus Christ "our hope," pointing to the resurrection and the promise of eternal life. The dual reference to both "God our Saviour" and "Lord Jesus Christ" affirms the full deity of both the Father and the Son.

---

### Verse 2
> **"Unto Timothy, my own son in the faith: Grace, mercy, and peace, from God our Father and Jesus Christ our Lord."**

**Context & Interpretation:**  
Paul addresses Timothy as "my own son in the faith," indicating their deep spiritual relationship. Paul led Timothy to Christ and mentored him. This is not a biological relationship but a spiritual one — Timothy is Paul's son in the Gospel. The greeting "grace, mercy, and peace" is Paul's expanded form of his usual "grace and peace." The addition of *mercy* here (unique to the Pastoral Epistles' greetings) is fitting for a young minister who needs not only God's favor but also His compassion in the face of ministry challenges.

---

### Verse 3
> **"As I besought thee to abide still at Ephesus, when I went into Macedonia, that thou mightest charge some that they teach no other doctrine,"**

**Context & Interpretation:**  
Paul reminds Timothy of the specific assignment he gave him: remain in Ephesus while Paul traveled to Macedonia. The word "charge" carries the weight of a military or official command. Timothy's primary mission was to correct doctrinal error. False teachers had infiltrated the Ephesian church, and Paul needed a trusted representative to confront them. This verse sets the pastoral context for the entire letter: it is a letter of instruction for dealing with error and establishing truth.

---

### Verse 4
> **"Neither give heed to fables and endless genealogies, which minister questions, rather than godly edifying which is in faith: so do."**

**Context & Interpretation:**  
Paul warns against giving attention to "fables" (myths, speculative stories) and "endless genealogies." These likely refer to elaborate Jewish speculations built around Old Testament genealogies, possibly combined with early Gnostic-type myths. Such teachings generated endless debate and controversy but produced no spiritual growth. The contrast is clear: these things "minister questions" (stir up speculation) rather than "godly edifying" (building up believers in faith). Sound doctrine builds up; false teaching tears down and distracts.

---

### Verse 5
> **"Now the end of the commandment is charity out of a pure heart, and of a good conscience, and of faith unfeigned:"**

**Context & Interpretation:**  
The word "end" here means the *goal* or *purpose*. The aim of Paul's charge — and indeed of all sound Christian teaching — is *charity* (love, the Greek *agape*). But Paul qualifies what genuine love looks like: it flows from (1) a **pure heart** — right inner motivations, (2) a **good conscience** — integrity and moral uprightness, and (3) **faith unfeigned** — sincere, genuine faith, not hypocritical religion. This verse is the positive standard against which the false teachers fall short.

---

### Verse 6
> **"From which some having swerved have turned aside unto vain jangling;"**

**Context & Interpretation:**  
"Swerved" (Greek: *astocheo*) means to miss the mark, to deviate from the target. The false teachers in Ephesus had drifted from the true goal of love, purity, and genuine faith. Their departure led them not into deeper truth but into "vain jangling" — empty, pointless chatter and argumentation. The Greek word (*mataiologia*) suggests meaningless, idle talk. This is a sober warning: when the heart is not right, theological discussion becomes mere noise.

---

### Verse 7
> **"Desiring to be teachers of the law; understanding neither what they say, nor whereof they affirm."**

**Context & Interpretation:**  
The false teachers *wanted* to be recognized as authoritative teachers of the Mosaic Law — a prestigious role in the Jewish-influenced religious culture of the day. But Paul exposes their fatal flaw: they do not understand what they are saying. There is a difference between wanting to teach and being qualified to teach. These men were confidently asserting things they could not actually explain or defend. Pride in religious knowledge without true understanding is a dangerous combination.

---

### Verse 8
> **"But we know that the law is good, if a man use it lawfully;"**

**Context & Interpretation:**  
Paul is careful not to dismiss the Law of God. The Law itself is good — it is holy, just, and given by God (Romans 7:12). The issue is not the Law but its *misuse*. The phrase "use it lawfully" is a wordplay in Greek (*nomimos*, meaning "according to its proper purpose"). When the Law is used for its intended purpose, it is beneficial. The problem in Ephesus was people using the Law in ways it was never intended — for speculation, pride, or for imposing Jewish legal requirements on Gentile believers.

---

### Verse 9
> **"Knowing this, that the law is not made for a righteous man, but for the lawless and disobedient, for the ungodly and for sinners, for unholy and profane, for murderers of fathers and murderers of mothers, for manslayers,"**

**Context & Interpretation:**  
The Law was not designed as an achievement system for the already-righteous. A person living in genuine righteousness by the Spirit does not need external legal threats to restrain sinful behavior. The Law's function is to expose and restrain sin. Paul lists categories of sinners in pairs: the *lawless and disobedient* (those who reject authority), the *ungodly and sinners* (those who reject God), the *unholy and profane* (those who disregard sacred things), and then specific extreme acts of violence. These groupings loosely mirror the Ten Commandments.

---

### Verse 10
> **"For whoremongers, for them that defile themselves with mankind, for menstealers, for liars, for perjured persons, and if there be any other thing that is contrary to sound doctrine;"**

**Context & Interpretation:**  
The list continues: *whoremongers* (sexually immoral persons), *them that defile themselves with mankind* (those who practice homosexual acts), and *menstealers* (slave traders, kidnappers — a direct violation of human dignity). *Liars* and *perjured persons* violate truth and the sanctity of oaths. The phrase "contrary to sound doctrine" is key — Paul is measuring all behavior against the standard of healthy, life-giving teaching. "Sound doctrine" (*hygiainousa didaskalia*) literally means "healthy teaching," doctrine that produces spiritual health in individuals and communities.

---

### Verse 11
> **"According to the glorious gospel of the blessed God, which was committed to my trust."**

**Context & Interpretation:**  
All the standards Paul has listed flow from "the glorious gospel of the blessed God." The Gospel — the good news of Jesus Christ — is the ultimate measure of all doctrine and conduct. Paul describes it as "glorious" (literally, "of glory") and as belonging to "the blessed God," emphasizing God's absolute happiness and fullness in Himself. Paul notes that this Gospel has been "committed to my trust" — a stewardship term. Paul did not own the Gospel; he was its custodian, responsible to guard and transmit it faithfully.

---

### Verse 12
> **"And I thank Christ Jesus our Lord, who hath enabled me, for that he counted me faithful, putting me into the ministry;"**

**Context & Interpretation:**  
The mention of the Gospel prompts Paul into a personal doxology of gratitude. He thanks Christ specifically — the one who *enabled* him (Greek: *endunamoo*, meaning to infuse with strength). Paul's ministry was not the product of his own talent or determination; it was Christ's empowerment. Equally significant: Christ "counted me faithful" — not that Paul was faithful before being called, but that God treated him as trustworthy in order to place him in service. This is grace: God calls and then equips.

---

### Verse 13
> **"Who was before a blasphemer, and a persecutor, and injurious: but I obtained mercy, because I did it ignorantly in unbelief."**

**Context & Interpretation:**  
Paul does not whitewash his past. Before his conversion, he was a *blasphemer* (he spoke against Christ and His name), a *persecutor* (he hunted down and imprisoned Christians), and *injurious* (violent, arrogant, one who outrages and harms others). These are severe self-descriptions. Yet he obtained mercy. The reason: "I did it ignorantly in unbelief." Paul is not making an excuse — ignorance does not eliminate sin — but explaining why mercy was possible. He had not sinned against light he had received; he was sincerely, if tragically, wrong. This distinguishes him from those who sin presumptuously and willfully.

---

### Verse 14
> **"And the grace of our Lord was exceeding abundant with faith and love which is in Christ Jesus."**

**Context & Interpretation:**  
God's response to Paul's sin was not minimal pardon — it was "exceeding abundant" grace. The Greek (*huperpleonazo*) is a compound intensifier meaning to overflow beyond measure, to superabound. Where sin was great, grace was greater still (Romans 5:20). This overflowing grace came *with* faith and love — the gifts that accompanied the grace. Faith to believe and love to be transformed are themselves expressions of divine grace, not human achievements. Everything Paul became was rooted in what God freely gave him in Christ.

---

### Verse 15
> **"This is a faithful saying, and worthy of all acceptation, that Christ Jesus came into the world to save sinners; of whom I am chief."**

**Context & Interpretation:**  
"Faithful saying" is a formula Paul uses in the Pastoral Epistles (see also 3:1; 4:9; 2 Tim. 2:11; Titus 3:8) to mark a statement as a recognized, trustworthy truth of the Christian community. The content here is the heart of the Gospel: *Christ Jesus came into the world to save sinners.* He came — implying pre-existence and intentional entry into humanity. He came *to save* — not merely to teach or inspire, but to rescue. He came to save *sinners* — not the righteous, not the deserving. Paul then makes the extraordinary claim: "of whom I am chief." He does not say "I was the chief sinner" (past tense) but "I am" — present tense — expressing ongoing humility even after years of faithful ministry.

---

### Verse 16
> **"Howbeit for this cause I obtained mercy, that in me first Jesus Christ might shew forth all longsuffering, for a pattern to them which should hereafter believe on him to life everlasting."**

**Context & Interpretation:**  
Paul reveals a divine purpose behind his own conversion: he was to be a *pattern* (Greek: *hupotyposis* — a sketch, a prototype, an example). If Christ could save Saul of Tarsus — the chief of sinners — then no one could ever claim to be beyond the reach of grace. Paul's life became a permanent exhibit of God's "all longsuffering" (complete patience). Every subsequent believer could look to Paul's story and find hope that God's grace is sufficient for them too. Salvation leads to "life everlasting" — not merely forgiveness, but an unending transformed existence in God.

---

### Verse 17
> **"Now unto the King eternal, immortal, invisible, the only wise God, be honour and glory for ever and ever. Amen."**

**Context & Interpretation:**  
Overwhelmed by the thought of God's mercy and grace, Paul breaks into a spontaneous doxology — a burst of praise. He addresses God with four majestic titles: (1) *King eternal* — ruler over all time and ages, (2) *immortal* — incorruptible, not subject to decay, (3) *invisible* — beyond human sight, dwelling in unapproachable light, (4) *the only wise God* — uniquely, supremely, and perfectly wise. To this God alone belong "honour and glory for ever and ever." The "Amen" seals the praise as certain and true. This is one of the great doxologies of the New Testament.

---

### Verse 18
> **"This charge I commit unto thee, son Timothy, according to the prophecies which went before on thee, that thou by them mightest war a good warfare;"**

**Context & Interpretation:**  
Returning from his doxology, Paul formally entrusts his charge to Timothy. The word "commit" (*paratithemi*) is a banking term — to deposit something for safekeeping. Paul is placing the solemn responsibility of guarding sound doctrine into Timothy's hands. He grounds this charge in "the prophecies which went before on thee" — apparently prophetic words spoken over Timothy at his commissioning (see also 4:14), confirming his calling. Paul then uses military language: "war a good warfare." Ministry is not passive; it requires courage, strategy, and endurance. Timothy is called to be a soldier of Christ.

---

### Verse 19
> **"Holding faith, and a good conscience; which some having put away concerning faith have made shipwreck:"**

**Context & Interpretation:**  
The weapons of this spiritual warfare are *faith* and *a good conscience*. These two always belong together: faith without a clean conscience becomes corrupt, and a troubled conscience undermines faith. The verb "holding" suggests actively clinging to something — it is a deliberate, ongoing effort. The warning that follows is sobering: "some having put away" a good conscience "have made shipwreck" of their faith. The Greek image is of a ship crashing onto rocks. Moral compromise always precedes doctrinal collapse. When people abandon conscience, their theology eventually follows.

---

### Verse 20
> **"Of whom is Hymenaeus and Alexander; whom I have delivered unto Satan, that they may learn not to blaspheme."**

**Context & Interpretation:**  
Paul names two specific individuals — Hymenaeus and Alexander — who had made shipwreck of their faith. Naming them is an act of public accountability and warning to the church. Paul says he has "delivered [them] unto Satan" — a severe act of church discipline (see also 1 Corinthians 5:5). This does not mean their damnation; the phrase refers to formal excommunication, removing them from the protection and fellowship of the church body and exposing them to the harsher realities of the world outside. The purpose is redemptive: "that they may learn not to blaspheme." Discipline is meant to correct, not destroy.

---

## Summary

1 Timothy 1 establishes the fundamental tension of true ministry: sound doctrine rooted in love, grace, and a clean conscience versus empty speculation, pride, and moral compromise. Paul grounds his authority in God's calling, his message in the glorious Gospel, and his humility in a candid acknowledgment of his own past. The chapter moves from warning (guard against false teaching) to testimony (God's grace is greater than the worst sin) to doxology (all praise to the eternal King) to charge (hold fast, fight the good fight). It is both a doctrinal foundation and a deeply personal letter from a spiritual father to his son.
